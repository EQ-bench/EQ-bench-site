The pavement glistened, a slick, dark mirror reflecting the bruised purple of the late October sky. Rain had fallen earlier, leaving the air sharp and clean, smelling of damp earth and decaying leaves – that particular London scent that always caught in my throat, a compound of melancholy and something strangely vital. My shoes, thin-soled things, really, quite unsuitable, tapped a hesitant rhythm against the stone flags. Tap-tap. Like a nervous bird pecking at a windowpane. Was I nervous? Perhaps. Or merely... adrift. Yes, adrift seemed the better word, carried along the currents of the street, the ebb and flow of hurried footsteps, the rumble of omnibuses like great, sighing beasts.

A splash of scarlet – a woman's scarf, impossibly bright against the grey – flared past, and for a moment, my breath hitched. Just the colour, of course. Not *her*. Not Clara, with her laughter like shattered glass and her careless way of breaking things. Hearts, mostly. Mine, once. Though that seemed a lifetime ago, buried under layers of subsequent days, subsequent greys. Still, the scarlet lingered on the retina, a phantom burn. How strange that colour could ambush one so. Like a scent – lilac, wasn't it, that Thomas always wore? A faint, clean smell that clung to his collar. Where was Thomas now? Married, they said. Children, perhaps. Living a solid, brick-built life somewhere south of the river, while I... I walked, watching the light fade.

The light. It pooled in puddles, trembled on the wet leaves clinging stubbornly to the railings of the square. A frail, watery gold, promising nothing, merely illuminating the decay with a kind of tender indifference. It caught the edge of a cloud, turning it momentarily into mother-of-pearl, soft and luminous, before the grey swallowed it again. One's thoughts, too, were like that – brief flashes of luminescence swallowed by the pervasive grey. Mother used to say I thought too much. ‘Eleanor, child, go out and *do* something,' she'd urge, her hands busy with her knitting, the needles clicking a relentless rhythm against the silence. But what was there to *do*, truly, except observe the light shifting, feel the cool air on one's skin, and try to piece together the fragments of... well, of everything?

My steps slowed before the small, steamed-up window of Mrs. Allen's shop. Stationery. Paper. Pens. The tools of capture, perhaps. An attempt to pin down the flickering thoughts, the elusive moments. Inside, the air was warm, smelling faintly of ink and old paper, a comforting, scholarly scent. Mrs. Allen, perched behind the counter like a small, wise owl, peered over her spectacles.

"Miss Harcourt. A raw day, isn't it?" Her voice was soft, dusty, like the undisturbed corners of her shop.

"Indeed, Mrs. Allen. Though the rain has held off." I ran a gloved finger over a stack of creamy writing paper. Thick, deckle-edged. The sort one used for important letters. Had I any important letters to write? Thomas... no. Clara... certainly not. Perhaps a note to Cecily, though what was there to say? *‘The weather here is grey. I walked by the river. "Just browsing today, then?" Mrs. Allen asked, her eyes, magnified by the lenses, holding a gentle, knowing quality. As if she saw the unwritten letters, the unspoken thoughts, hovering around me like hesitant ghosts.

"I... I thought perhaps some ink. A different shade. Something... less predictable than blue-black." My own voice sounded thin, uncertain. Why did I feel so exposed under her mild gaze? As if she could see the indecision, the formlessness of my days.

She nodded slowly, reaching under the counter. "Perhaps... this?" She produced a small, square bottle. The label read: ‘Forest Green'.

Forest Green. I tilted the bottle. It looked almost black in the dim light, but held up towards the window, it revealed a deep, mossy heart. Green. The colour of hidden places, of things growing in shadow. Not the vibrant green of spring, but the deeper, more complex green of late summer, hinting at the turn towards autumn. It reminded me of the woods behind Aunt Mary's house, where I'd hidden as a child, convinced I could disappear entirely among the ferns and tangled roots. A place of refuge.

"Yes," I heard myself say, the word surprisingly firm. "Yes, that's lovely. Thank you, Mrs. Allen."

She wrapped the bottle carefully in brown paper, twisting the ends with practiced fingers. "There we are. Something to make your mark with."

Make my mark. The phrase echoed as I stepped back out into the grey afternoon, the little paper-wrapped bottle cool and solid in my hand. Make my mark. But on what? On whom? The creamy paper remained unbought on Mrs. Allen's shelf. The letters remained unwritten. Thomas's solid life, Clara's scarlet vibrancy, Mother's relentless knitting – they had all made their marks, hadn't they? While I... I collected shades of ink. Forest Green.

The streetlights were beginning to hum, casting hazy halos in the damp air. Faces swam past, anonymous, each closed within its own world of thoughts, worries, destinations. Were they all heading towards solid, brick-built lives? Or were some, like me, merely adrift, watching the light change, clutching a small bottle of Forest Green ink as if it held some secret answer? The river, only a few streets away now, would be a dark, sliding ribbon, carrying forgotten things towards the sea. Perhaps I would walk that way. Yes. A reflection of one's own smallness, one's own quiet drift upon the surface of things. The tapping of my shoes seemed less hesitant now, absorbed into the deeper rhythm of the city settling into evening. Tap-tap. Forest Green. The light fading. Adrift. Yes.
