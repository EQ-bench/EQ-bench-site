# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Virginia Woolf: Write a slice of story that takes place over the course of a single day in a character's life. Use stream-of-consciousness narration to explore the character's thoughts and perceptions. Include poetic, impressionistic descriptions of the character's surroundings and sensations. Weave in memories and reflections that provide insight into the character's past and inner life. The scene should feel like part of a lived-in world, with the scene naturally existing in a wider story. 800-1000 words, first-person, past tense.

# The Hours Between Us

The morning light slipped through the gap in the curtains, a slender finger of gold that touched my eyelids, and I awoke to the sound of the city stirring beyond my window. How curious that time should start again this way, that consciousness should return like an unwelcome guest, bringing with it the weight of yesterday and all the yesterdays before. I lay there, watching dust motes dance in the beam of light, each one a tiny universe spinning, spinning, spinning away from me.

I rose, my feet finding the cool wooden floorboards that creaked in their familiar places. Mrs. Dalloway would buy the flowers herself. The thought came unbidden, a fragment from a book I had read last summer in the garden at Bourton, when the roses were in bloom and Edmund had still looked at me as though I were the answer to a question he had been asking all his life. How distant that afternoon seemed now, separated from this moment by an ocean of days, each one bleeding into the next like watercolors on damp paper.

The kettle whistled, a high, thin sound that pierced the morning stillness. Outside my window, London was awakening--a symphony of car horns and voices, of footsteps hurrying along pavement, of lives intersecting and diverging again. I poured the water over the tea leaves and watched them unfurl, releasing their essence into the hot water. Like memories, I thought, that expand and color everything around them when stirred.

"You always were fanciful," Mother would have said, her mouth a tight line of disapproval. How she had hated my tendency to drift, to see connections between disparate things. "A practical mind is a woman's greatest asset," she would insist, as though imagination were a liability, a dangerous current that might carry me away from the safe harbor of convention.

The tea was too hot, burning my tongue, and I welcomed the small pain, anchoring me to the present moment. I carried my cup to the window and looked out at the park across the street, where the trees were just beginning to surrender to autumn, their leaves edged with gold and copper. A young woman pushed a pram along the path, her head bent against the breeze. Had she slept last night? Did she too feel the weight of each hour, the relentless forward motion of time?

I dressed carefully, choosing a blue dress that Edmund had once said brought out the color of my eyes. "Like the sea at Bourton," he had murmured, his fingers tracing the line of my jaw. Why did I still dress for him, when he was gone? Three years since the telegram came, three years of waking to the absence of him, and still I chose this dress, as though he might appear at my door, his smile lighting his face as it had that summer.

The gallery was quiet when I arrived, the paintings hanging in their perfect silence, waiting for eyes to find them. I moved through the rooms, my heels clicking against the marble floors, echoing in the vastness. There was the Turner that I had come to see, a storm at sea, the waves and sky merging in a tumult of light and darkness. I stood before it, lost in the chaos of the canvas, feeling myself dissolve into the paint, into the storm itself.

"It's quite remarkable, isn't it?" A voice beside me, and I turned to find a woman, perhaps my age, perhaps older--it was difficult to tell, for her face had the timeless quality of someone who has suffered and survived.

"Yes," I replied, my voice sounding strange to my ears, as though it belonged to someone else. "It makes me think of..." But I could not articulate what it made me think of--Edmund, perhaps, or the way life can change in an instant, how calm waters can become tempestuous without warning.

She nodded as though I had completed the thought, as though she understood what I could not say. "I come here often," she said. "The paintings never change, but I do. Each time I see something different."

We stood together in companionable silence, two strangers united by a moment of shared appreciation. How strange, I thought, that we should be here, now, looking at this painting, our lives momentarily converging like streams joining a river. What confluence of events had brought us to this precise point in time and space?

Later, as the afternoon light began to fade, I walked by the river, watching the water move beneath the bridges, dark and swift. A memory surfaced: Edmund and I on the river at Bourton, the boat rocking gently beneath us, his hand warm on mine. "I will love you forever," he had said, and I had believed him, for in that moment, forever seemed possible, time stretching out before us like the river, endless and full of promise.

The evening brought a fine mist that softened the edges of buildings and turned streetlights into halos of amber light. I made my way home, carrying with me the Turner, the stranger at the gallery, the river flowing toward the sea. In my flat, I lit a lamp and sat by the window, watching the night gather itself around the city.

Tomorrow would come, as it always did, with its own demands and revelations. But for now, there was this moment, this breath, this consciousness that contained multitudes--Edmund and Mother, Bourton and London, past and present flowing together like the river, like time itself, carrying me forward into the darkness and the light.
