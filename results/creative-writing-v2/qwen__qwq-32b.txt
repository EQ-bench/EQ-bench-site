"He left a note," she continued, as though the admission required no reply. "Said he was ‘too restless to stay.'" Her fingers twisted the edge of her coat. "You always had a way with words. Do you think it's true?"

Outside, the wind shifted, carrying the cry of a distant boat's bell. My fingers itched to fetch the journal from the shelf, the one where I'd written of such things, but that would have been intimate, and Ada was neither friend nor confidante. The sea now was calmer, its surface a mirror fractured by the wind. I dipped my hand into the water, the cold biting through skin that had forgotten how to shiver. Ada's words echoed--*too restless to stay*--and I wondered if it were as simple as that. Or if the restlessness was a hunger, a void that no shore could fill.  

By dusk, the light had turned to liquid gold, spilling over the horizon. I stood on the balcony, the day's events unraveling like the last threads of sunlight. Ada, with her brittle certainty, the boy's laughter, the ghost of my brother's voice--each a stitch in the tapestry of my solitude.  

The tides, I realized, were never the same twice. They retreated, returned, carved new channels in the sand. Like us, they carried what they could not hold--the memories of storms, the salt of tears, the fragments of lives once intertwined.
