# My response 
Man, I can hear how draining this has become for you. You’re putting real energy—money, time, brainpower—into trying to help, and when it ends in an argument it feels like none of that effort counts. Anybody would feel fed‑up in your shoes.

Can I offer a thought that’s half relationship, half psychology? For a lot of people—especially women who’ve been judged on their bodies since middle school—weight talk isn’t just math; it’s wrapped up in self‑worth. When you lay out the calorie numbers, even gently, it can land like a spotlight on her shame, not like helpful data. So while your facts are solid, the delivery might be colliding with a very raw nerve.

What usually works better is shifting from “coach” to “teammate.” Instead of monitoring her intake, maybe ask, “What kind of support actually feels good to you right now—accountability, doing active things together, or maybe just listening when you’re frustrated?” That puts the control back in her hands and signals you’re safe, not judging.

Also—and this is tricky—separate her goal from your peace of mind. If she decides she’s not ready to change yet, that’s her body and her choice. You get to decide how you cope with the complaints: you can say, “I love you, and I’m here when you want to brainstorm solutions, but hearing daily negativity leaves me feeling helpless. Could we set aside specific times to talk about it?” That way you’re not the food police, and you still protect your own sanity.

You’ve already proven you can be disciplined; now the challenge is using that discipline to hold empathy instead of spreadsheets. If she ever asks for technical help, you’re loaded with knowledge. Until then, the most loving—and ironically the most effective—thing may be to step back, affirm her autonomy, and keep showing up as partner rather than project manager.
