He’s cornered the narrative: *Look at me, look at me now.* The crowd’s energy is fuel, and he’s feeding off it.  

Inside, though, he’s grappling with vertigo. The initial adrenaline is peaking, and a question lingers: *Was this too far?* But doubt is drowned out by the roar of his own voice through the megaphone. He’s reciting stats, poems, curses—he’s *performing* to cement his legacy. The cops’ hesitation (maybe they’re waiting for orders?) only feeds his thrill.  

He called now because he wants validation but also… needs an exit strategy. He’s not a martyr; he’ll burn out fast. The cuffs are a gimmick, but they’ve painted him into a corner. He’s craving reassurance that this was worth it, that the world is watching.  

There’s a flicker of fear too. What if they drag him off? What if the media misrepresents him? But his ego won’t let him admit it. He’s in “the zone”—a place where fear becomes fuel. The call’s a test: *Is my friend still with me?*  

Deep down, he’s terrified of being forgotten. This stunt is his bid to be unforgettable, but the cuffs are a silent plea: *“Help me turn this into something bigger.”*
