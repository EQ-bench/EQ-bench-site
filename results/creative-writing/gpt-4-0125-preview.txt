Fairy Tale Retelling: Rewrite the story of Hansel and Gretel from the perspective of the witch, in the format of raw, terse stream-of-consciousness diary entries written in her style & voice. She may at times be an unreliable narrator. She sees herself as fundamentally good and portrays herself sympathetically; she believes she is misunderstood and has a tragic backstory. Include snippets of dialogue between the witch and the children in a way that feels natural for a diary entry. You may take liberties with the original story. The witch will not die in this version; she needs to be able to write her final entry. It will not be happily ever after. 600-800 words.

**Day 1**

They came today. Two children, crumbs and desperation trailing behind them like a shadow. I saw them from my window, their eyes wide, hands clutching each other. Lost. Or so I thought. I should've known, should've seen the glint in their eyes. But no, I saw only their hunger, their need. And something in me stirred, something old and forgotten. A memory of my own hunger, my own need.

I invited them in. "Poor dears," I said, "you must be starving." And they were, oh, how they were. They ate as if they'd never seen food. And I, foolish I, felt a flicker of warmth, of pride. To provide, to nurture. It's what I've always longed for, isn't it? To be seen as something other than the monster they whisper about in the village.

**Day 3**

They've eaten me out of house and home. Not that I mind, not really. It's nice, isn't it? To be needed. And they do need me, even if they don't say it. Even if they whisper when they think I'm not listening. "She's not what she seems," the boy said today. "We need to be careful." Careful of what, dear boy? Of kindness? Of generosity?

I've offered them a bed, a roof, safety. And yet, they plot. I've heard them, late at night, scheming. They don't understand. They can't. How could they know what it's like to be so alone, to be so... hungry for something more than food?

**Day 5**

Today was... difficult. The girl, she's clever, too clever by half. "Why are your walls made of candy?" she asked, her eyes too knowing. "And why is your window of sugar glass?" Oh, child, if only you knew. If only I could tell you about the loneliness that eats at you, about the need to make something, anything, beautiful. To create a world where no one is hungry, where no one is alone.

But I said nothing. I smiled, a brittle, sugar-glass smile. "It's to make the world sweeter, my dear." She didn't believe me. I saw it in her eyes. The mistrust, the fear. It's always the same. No matter how hard I try, it's always the same.

**Day 7**

The boy, he tried to run today. "We're leaving," he declared, bold as brass. But where will you go, little one? Back to the parents who left you in the woods? Back to a world that doesn't want you? I couldn't let him, couldn't let them leave. Not yet. They don't understand. They need me. They just don't know it yet.

I told them about the garden, about the fruits that never rot, the flowers that never wilt. "You can stay here," I said. "You can be happy." But the boy, he shook his head. "We don't belong here," he said. And it cut, deeper than I thought possible. Because isn't that what I've always wanted? To belong? To be more than the whispers, the fear?

**Day 10**

It's over. They're gone. They outsmarted me, the clever little things. Found a way to break the locks, to escape my garden of eternal bloom. I should be angry, I suppose. I should rage and scream and curse them. But all I feel is this hollow emptiness, this aching void.

They didn't understand. They couldn't. I only wanted to protect them, to keep them safe. From hunger, from loneliness, from a world that's too harsh, too cruel. But they've chosen that world over mine. They've chosen reality over the sweet, sugar-glass illusion I offered.

**Final Entry**

The house is quiet now, too quiet. The garden blooms, untouched. The candy walls glisten in the sun, mocking me with their sweetness. I thought I could be different. I thought I could be the hero of my own story. But in the end, I'm just the witch in the woods, alone with her candy and her regrets.

They'll tell their story, the children. They'll speak of the wicked witch and her gingerbread house. Of their bravery, their cunning. And they'll be right, won't they? Because no matter how I try to spin it, to sweeten the tale, I'm still the monster in the end.

But perhaps that's all I was ever meant to be. A warning, a lesson. Stay on the path, children. Beware the sweetness that hides the poison. Beware the witch who offers you shelter, for she is more alone, more hungry, than you can ever imagine.

And so, I write this final entry, not as a plea for understanding or forgiveness. No, I write this as a goodbye. To the children, to the world, to the part of me that still dreamed of something more. Goodbye, dear diary. May my story be a cautionary tale, a bittersweet morsel in a world that craves only the sugar-coated truth.

Tomorrow, I would face another day of training, another day of fighting for my life. But for now, I would rest, drawing strength from the gods and from the dream of a life yet to be lived.

# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. Include dialogue that is terse and revealing of character. Write with a keen sense of time and place. Capture realness and authenticity. 600-800 words, past tense.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
The bar was small and the light was dim. It was the kind of place that seemed to absorb sound, making every conversation a private affair. The walls were lined with photographs of people who had passed through, some smiling, some not. The soldier sat at the bar, his back straight, a glass of whiskey in front of him. He hadn't touched it yet. His hands were rough, the skin on his knuckles scarred from fights long forgotten. His eyes, though, they told the story of battles that were far more recent.

A man sat down beside him, ordered a beer, and looked at the soldier. "You just get back?" he asked, nodding towards the uniform that the soldier hadn't bothered to change out of yet.

"Yeah," the soldier replied, his voice flat.

"Must be nice," the man said, taking a swig of his beer.

The soldier finally took a sip of his whiskey. "Depends on what you mean by nice."

The man laughed, a short, harsh sound. "I guess you're right. Hell, I wouldn't know. Never served."

The soldier looked at him, really looked at him, for the first time. "You don't have to serve to know that coming back isn't the hard part. It's figuring out what to do once you're here."

The bartender, a woman in her fifties with hair the color of steel wool, came over and refilled the soldier's glass. "On the house," she said, with a nod that was both acknowledgment and respect.

"Thanks," the soldier said, but he didn't smile.

The man next to him was quiet for a moment, then he said, "What's your plan?"

"No plan," the soldier said. "Just trying to get used to the idea that I don't have to wake up at dawn and worry about getting shot at before breakfast."

"That's something, at least," the man said, trying to sound encouraging.

The soldier shrugged. "Maybe."

They sat in silence for a while, the only sounds the low hum of conversation from the other patrons and the occasional clink of glassware. The soldier's gaze drifted to a photograph above the bar, a group of young men in uniform, smiling and carefree. He wondered where they were now.

A young woman approached the bar, her laughter like a bell in the quiet room. She ordered a drink, her eyes bright and full of life. She glanced at the soldier, then did a double take. "Hey, aren't you Johnny's brother?"

The soldier turned to her, a flicker of recognition in his eyes. "Yeah, that's me."

"I thought so! Johnny talks about you all the time. Says you're a hero."

The soldier's mouth twisted into something that might have been a smile, but it didn't reach his eyes. "Johnny's got a big mouth."

The woman laughed, oblivious to the tension. "Well, it's good to have you back. The whole town's been talking about it."

"Great," the soldier said, not sounding like he thought it was great at all.

The woman seemed to sense she'd said the wrong thing. "I just mean... it's good, you know? That you're safe."

"Yeah," the soldier said, turning back to his drink. "Safe."

The woman lingered for a moment longer, then, with a final, uncertain smile, she walked away.

The man beside the soldier watched her go, then turned back to him. "You don't seem too happy to be back."

The soldier looked at his whiskey, watching the light play off the amber liquid. "I'm not sure I know how to be happy about it yet. Maybe I will be, in time. For now, it's enough to sit here and drink and not think about anything else."

The man nodded, as if he understood. "Well, here's to not thinking about anything else."

They raised their glasses in a silent toast, the whiskey burning a path down their throats, a temporary balm for wounds that were far from healed. The soldier's gaze was distant, lost in thoughts of a past that was too close and a future that was still out of reach. But for a moment, in that small, dimly lit bar, he found a semblance of peace.
