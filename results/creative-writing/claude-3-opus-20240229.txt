# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. #####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
The bar was dark and smoky, the kind of place a man could get lost in. I sat at the counter, nursing a whiskey, straight. The bartender knew me, knew not to ask questions. He just kept the drinks coming.

I heard the door open behind me, felt the draft of cold night air. Footsteps approached. A man slid onto the stool next to mine. I glanced over. He had the look, the same one I saw in the mirror each morning. Eyes that had seen too much. The weariness in the set of his shoulders.

"Whiskey, neat," he told the bartender. His voice was gravel, ground down by the weight of the world.

The bartender set the drink in front of him. The man threw it back in one practiced motion. He signaled for another.

"Just get back?" I asked, staring straight ahead.

"Yeah. Two days ago." He took a sip of his refill. "You?"

"A week. Feels longer."

He grunted. "It always does."

We drank in silence for a while, lost in our own thoughts. The memories never really left you. They lurked, waiting to ambush you in the quiet moments.

"Where were you stationed?" he asked.

"France, mostly. Some time in Belgium at the end."

"Pacific theater for me. Okinawa."

I nodded. We'd all had our own slice of hell.

He rubbed at a scar on his forearm, absentminded. The mark was raised and angry looking, like a brand. Shrapnel, most likely.

"Souvenir?" I asked, gesturing with my glass.

He glanced down, like he'd forgotten it was there. "Something like that. Got it in my first engagement. Thought I was going to die right there in the dirt."

"But you didn't."

"No. I didn't." He said it like he still couldn't quite believe it.

I understood. Some days, being alive felt more surreal than the war ever had. Like any minute you'd wake up back in the thick of it, bullets whizzing past your ear, the taste of mud and blood in your mouth.

"I keep thinking it'll get easier," I said. "Being back."

"Does it?"

"Not so far." I drained my glass, signaled for another. "I don't know what to do with myself. It's like I've forgotten how to just...live."

"I know what you mean." He scratched at his stubble, a few days past a clean shave. "I feel like a stranger in my own skin. Like I'm walking around in someone else's life."

"Everybody wants things to go back to normal. But I don't even know what that means anymore."

"There is no normal. Not after what we've seen." He shook his head. "The wife, she tries to understand. But she can't. No one can."

"To being a stranger," I said, raising my glass in a mock toast.

He clinked his against it. "To being a stranger."

We drank. The whiskey burned going down, a familiar fire. Outside, the night stretched on, dark and endless. But in here, in this dingy little bar, we'd found a moment of solace. A brief reprieve from the ghosts that haunted us.

The bartender wiped down the counter, pretending not to listen. He'd heard it all before, I'm sure. The bar was full of men like us, stumbling our way through a world that no longer made sense.

"What now?" the soldier asked. It could have meant tonight, or the rest of our lives. 

I shrugged. "Same thing we've always done, I suppose. Survive."

He huffed a laugh, short and bitter. "Survive," he echoed. "I guess that's all we can do."

We lapsed back into silence, each alone with our demons. But for a little while, at least, we didn't have to face them by ourselves. In the shared stillness, there was a strange sort of peace. The kind that exists between men who have walked through the valley of the shadow and come out the other side.

The bartender left the bottle. We drank, and remembered, and tried our best to forget. Tomorrow would come, as it always did. But for tonight, this was enough.
